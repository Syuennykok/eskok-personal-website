# Basics of Research

- definition of research
	- making **novel and significant** contributions to our understanding of the world, based on reproducible observations and verifiable results
- Differences between **Science** and **Engineering**
	- Science: discovering new theories, paradigms, approaches, algorithms, simulations, experiments, ....
	- Engineering: solve real-world problems with new technologies, designs, processes, methods, models, testing ......
- Keys: Novelty and Significance
	- Novelty: unknown previously
	- Significance: bring huge benefit to many people in the world
- 11 tasks of researchers
	- Explore and come up with new ideas
	- Validate and implement the new ideas to see if they work, and make them work well
	- Write up the research outcome
	- Review papers and manuscripts from other researchers to see if they are novel and significant enough to be accepted for publication
	- Administrate academic journals and organize academic conferences, especially when one becomes an established researcher
	- Attend academic conferences to present papers, to learn the most recent research results, and to exchange research ideas and knowledge with other researchers
	- Apply for research grants from government and other organizations to support research, including providing support to graduate students
	- Supervise graduate students
	- Teach graduate and undergraduate courses in universities and colleges
	- Perform administrative work for the organizations that researchers work in
	- Commercialize research outcome
- Most important skills and abilities for researchers
	- Passion, focus, enthusiasm, and interests in research
	- Curiosity and creativity
	- Critical and independent thinking
	- Risk taking
	- High scientific integrity
	- Quick learning and strong analytical and problem-solving skills
	- Diligences
	- Good communication skills
- Pros to become a researchers
	- High career satisfaction
	- Protected research environment
	- Reasonably good income
	- Researchers are highly respected by most societies
- Cons to become a researchers
	- Researchers often work more than 8 hours a day and 40 hours a week to stay competitive
	- Long preparation time
	- Relatively narrow career choice

# Goals of Ph.D. Research

- **Goals 1:** Be the best in the field
	- Be focus!
	- No shortcut in producing highly novel and significant work with little effort, or knowing related areas
-  **Goals 2:** Be independent
- Three key tasks
	- find new ideas
	- do solid research
	- publish top papers
- Distractions to the GOALS
	- language barrier, culture shock, homesickness .....

# Getting Started: Finding New Ideas and Organizing Your Plans

- First year
	- taking several broad, graduate-level courses that are related to your future research or do related readings
	- explore a few interesting topics

## How to find relevant papers

Some guidelines:
- highly reputable journals and conferences review and select papers very rigorously
- impact factor
- senior and well-established researchers
- use scholarly search engine

## How to read papers

- quickly understand the problems, assumptions, main ideas and the proposed solution presented in the paper at a high-level
- spend more time to analyze the paper critically, find new ideas, and conduct research to surpass the previous papers
- 30% effort reading and understanding the paper; 70% effort thinking creatively and critically
- need-driven learning


## How to know if I am on the right track

- Discuss your potential topic with your supervisor and other colleagues and Ph.D. students extensively
- Discuss your ideas with other researcher who work in the same field via email
- Chat with other researchers in the conferences
- Find and read recent Ph.D. theses on similar topics from other universities, and compare the depth and scope of your research work to theirs

## Setting up a plan

- when?
	- after you have gone through task 1-3 once or twice
- remain flexible with the plan
- strong focus

# Conducting Solid Research

## Overview of research process

1. Find a novel and potentially high-impact problem to solve based on the high-level criteria on choosing a good problem
2. Find a candidate solution
3. Conduct theoretical or empirical analysis on the hypotheses to confirm or to dispel them. You can also conduct significance tests to establish the validity of your conclusions on these hypotheses.
4. If your are successful in your significance testing, then you are done. Otherwise, you return to Step 2, find another candidate solution to the problem, and repeat the process.

## Jim Gray's Criteria

He stated that good research should:
- have clear benefit
- be simple to state
- have no obvious solution
- have a criterion where progress and solutions are testable. and where it is possible to break the larger problem down into smaller steps, so that one can see intermediate progress after each step.

## Empirical VS Theoretical research

SCIENCE: *Theoretical* VS *Empirical*
ENGINEERING: *Fundamental* VS *Application-oriented*

- Empirical research
	- system building, gaining experiences, making observations......
	- hypotheses and empirical research often tightly coupled
	- How to get ideas?
		- learning by repeating some other researcher's work and rebuilding their systems
		- understand what was left out of others work
		- important resource: baseline or benchmark system
- Theoretical research
	- proposes models and hypotheses that can be generalized and tested
	- good theories should have the ability to deduce new facts about the future
	- Two purposes
		- for experiments to confirm or disprove a preceding theory
		- vice versa - experiments before theory
